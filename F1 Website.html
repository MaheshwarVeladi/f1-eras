2014
2014 – 2020

The Silver
Dominance

The 2014 regulation overhaul — moving from 2.4L V8s to 1.6L V6 turbocharged hybrid power units — handed Mercedes an insurmountable advantage. They had mastered the complex energy recovery systems while rivals struggled.

  • ⚙
    New Power Unit Architecture 1.6L V6 turbo + MGU-H + MGU-K replaced the 2.4L V8s. Teams had to develop entirely new powertrains — and Mercedes was years ahead.
  • 🔋
    Energy Recovery Systems (ERS) Two motor generator units harvested energy from braking (MGU-K) and exhaust heat (MGU-H), delivering up to 160bhp of electrical assistance.
  • 📉
    Token System (2015–2016) A controversial system limited how many PU components teams could develop per season — designed to control costs, it locked in existing performance gaps.

Drivers' Championships

Lewis Hamilton claimed five of the seven WDC titles in this era (2014, 2015, 2017, 2018, 2019). Nico Rosberg won in 2016 and retired the following morning. The sport had never seen sustained dominance like this.

The stat that defined an era

Between 2014 and 2020, Mercedes started 117 races and took 112 pole positions. In 2016 — their least dominant year — they still won 19 of 21 races.

2021
Season 2021

The Fight
That Changed
Everything

Red Bull and Adrian Newey had finally built a car to match Mercedes. Max Verstappen and Lewis Hamilton traded blows across 22 races — incidents in Silverstone, Monza, Jeddah — arriving at Abu Dhabi level on points. What followed became the most controversial final lap in F1 history.

  • ✂
    Aerodynamic Tweaks Floor edge and diffuser regulations were tightened, specifically targeting Mercedes' low-rake concept. Red Bull's high-rake design was largely unaffected.
  • 💰
    Budget Cap Introduced A $145M per-team cost cap came into force for the first time, beginning F1's transformation from spending wars to engineering efficiency.
  • ⚠
    Abu Dhabi — The Finale A Safety Car restart decision by Race Director Michael Masi allowed lapped cars to unlap, creating a one-lap shootout. Verstappen passed Hamilton. The world erupted.

Race Director Michael Masi was removed from his role in the off-season. A new Virtual Race Control room was established. And a 26-year-old Dutchman had announced himself as the sport's new dominant force.

2022
2022 – 2025

The Ground
Effect
Revolution

The most radical regulation overhaul since 1983. F1 abandoned complex multi-element wings and re-introduced ground effect — generating downforce from beneath the car rather than above it. The reset promised closer racing. Red Bull mastered it immediately.

  • ↓
    Ground Effect Aerodynamics Venturi tunnels under the floor generate suction, pressing the car to the track. Less turbulent wake means cars can follow each other more closely — improving overtaking.
  • ⭕
    18-Inch Wheels Switching from 13-inch to 18-inch rims brought F1 closer to road car technology. The new low-profile Pirelli tyres changed the handling characteristics entirely.
  • 🏗
    Simplified Aerodynamic Package Complex bargeboards, multi-element front wings, and intricate turning vanes were banned. Simpler surfaces — but the devil was in the underfloor details.
  • 📊
    Budget Cap Tightened to $140M Teams caught breaching the cap faced sporting and financial penalties. Red Bull received a $7M fine and 10% CFD reduction in 2022 for a minor breach.

Red Bull's Dominance

Max Verstappen won 15 of 22 races in 2022 — a record at the time. The RB18, designed by Adrian Newey, was so dominant that rivals spent the entire season copying its concepts rather than developing their own cars.

The porpoising problem

The return of ground effect brought an unexpected problem — porpoising, a violent bouncing caused by the floor alternately stalling and reattaching. Mercedes suffered the most; Red Bull had solved it before the season started.

2024
2024 – 2025

The Papaya
Takeover

After Red Bull's historic 2023 season — 19 wins from 22 races — the world expected more of the same. Instead, a resurgent McLaren rewrote the script across two seasons, ending Red Bull's stranglehold on both championships and crowning a new generation of champions.

2024 — The Shift Begins

After Red Bull's historical 2023 season in the RB19 ended with Max Verstappen's third championship title with an infallible win rate of 86.36%, winning 19 out of 22 races, people expected 2024 to go the same way. However, a resurgent McLaren seemed to have other plans in mind. While they failed to prevent Verstappen from clinching his fourth consecutive championship victory, McLaren won the ultimate title that every manufacturer aimed for — the World Constructors' Championship.

2025 — Year of the Papayas

McLaren began the 2025 season with a thunderous start, clinching two of the three podium positions in Australia. Oscar Piastri dominated the majority of the season, with Lando Norris playing catch-up the whole while. A controversial position swap between the McLaren drivers and Red Bull's new rear wing breathed life into the title fight. As Piastri suffered failures from Baku onwards, Norris drove to the top. Then Verstappen launched the greatest comeback the sport had witnessed — a 100% podium rate from Zandvoort, shaving a 100-point gap to a mere 2 points at the season finale. The title fell to Lando Norris.

2026
2026 — Incoming

The Next
Chapter

The most ambitious regulation set in F1 history is coming. New power units, active aerodynamics, lighter cars, and a completely reshuffled manufacturer landscape. Every team starts from zero again. History says the first to solve it wins for a decade.

  • ⚡
    New Power Unit — MGU-H Removed The complex Motor Generator Unit-Heat is eliminated, simplifying the hybrid system. But the MGU-K is massively uprated — electric power rises from ~160bhp to ~350bhp. Nearly 50% of total power will be electrical.
  • 🔄
    Active Aerodynamics For the first time since 1983, moveable aerodynamic surfaces return. Automated systems will open the front and rear wings on straights to reduce drag, closing them automatically in corners.
  • 🏭
    New Manufacturers Enter Audi joins as a full works team. Ford partners Red Bull Powertrains. Honda returns to supply the new Red Bull engine. The PU supplier landscape is almost unrecognisable from 2025.

Sustainable Fuel

100% sustainable fuel becomes mandatory — a major step in F1's carbon neutrality commitment.

📐

Shorter Cars

Cars shrink in length and width — addressing criticism that current cars are too large to race closely.

The question everyone is asking

Every major regulation change has produced a new dominant team. 2014 gave us Mercedes. 2022 gave us Red Bull. Who cracks the 2026 code first — and will the rest of the grid close the gap faster than ever before, thanks to the budget cap?

Already causing controversy

Mercedes and Red Bull have publicly disagreed over the active aero specifications. Several teams claim the 2026 car concepts will be nearly undriveable at low speed due to the aero balance shift when the front wing opens.
